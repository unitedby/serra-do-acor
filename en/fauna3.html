Serra do Açor

Fauna

PT

EN

The biotopes that make up this Protected Area support a very diverse fauna with the occurrence of certain Iberian endemics that increase its ecological value.
Among the most important biotopes for fauna are broadleaf forests, riparian corridors, scrublands, agricultural areas and olive groves.
The biotopes that host the greatest number of fauna species, and the most important species for conservation, are the native broadleaf communities, followed by the riparian communities.

Reptiles

The 11 reptile species present in the Serra do Açor Protected Landscape are all protected by the Bern Convention.

Particularly noteworthy are Schreiber’s green lizard (Lacerta schreiberi), a species also included in Annexes B-II and B-IV of the Habitats Directive, and Lataste’s viper (Vipera latastei), a species classified as Vulnerable according to the Red Book of the Vertebrates of Portugal.

Schreiber’s green lizard (Lacerta schreiberi) is a reptile endemic to the Iberian Peninsula, distributed mainly in areas with a strong Atlantic climatic influence in the northwest of the peninsula, although it is also present in some isolated southern populations. In Portugal it occurs almost continuously north of the Tagus, except for the coastal areas between Leiria and the Tagus River, where it appears in fragmented form. In the south it occurs in four distinct and isolated population nuclei: Serra de Monchique, Serra do Cercal, Serra de São Mamede and Ponte de Sor. In the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation it is broadly distributed. Indeed, the whole SAC has the potential to provide the species with suitable biotope areas. It is found preferably along the banks of permanent watercourses with well-developed riparian vegetation. It is considered a very selective species in habitat terms. The main threat factors include frequent stabilization works along the banks of watercourses, extraction of inert materials, cleaning of riparian margins, and water pollution, which are responsible for the degradation and reduction of suitable habitat. (ICNF, 2019). (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Invertebrates

The invertebrate species are divided into the following taxonomic groups:

120 aquatic macroinvertebrate species from Mata da Margaraça streams; 61 terrestrial springtail species; 241 butterfly species.

Some species are protected at the international level, such as the stag beetle (Lucanus cervus), the marsh fritillary (Euphydryas aurinia), Gomphus graslinii, Euplagia quadripunctaria, Oxygastra curtisii, and Macromia splendens. The stag beetle (Lucanus cervus) is the largest beetle in Portugal and one of the largest insects in Europe. Males can reach 8 cm in length. It is reddish-brown and shows marked sexual dimorphism (males and females are visibly different), with the male’s head and mandibles much larger than the female’s and these are used for territorial defence. It prefers oak woods and other deciduous forests (where the trees lose their leaves and become bare), but it is also found in riparian corridors, orchards, olive groves and gardens, always in association with old trees. Adults feed on fruit and plant sap, and the larvae feed on rotting wood. It is best observed in spring and summer.

Euphydryas
aurinia

This is a common species in Portugal, occurring in most of mainland Portugal.

It is present in the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation, probably occurring across much of the SAC in patches with suitable biotopes (ICNF, 2019). The species’ preferred habitat includes biotopes with a certain degree of moisture, where its host plants develop (Lonicera periclymenum and L. etrusca, Succisa pratensis, Plantago lanceolata, Scabiosa spp.). It is found in humid meadows, peat bogs, fallow land, and verges along paths (Maravalhas, 2003; ICNF, 2006). In general, it requires a mosaic of open woodland (for breeding) and meadows (for the adult phase). This structure is maintained through traditional management. The main threats are associated with loss of connectivity between suitable habitat areas, which may result from the destruction of native vegetation and colonization by invasive non-native species or by fire (which acts both by directly destroying individuals and by destroying host plants).
Agricultural intensification, drainage, and the filling of wetlands have also been identified as factors responsible for the destruction of suitable habitat and the consequent fragmentation and isolation of populations. In principle, it is not threatened in Portugal, but it is declining in urban and coastal areas, where human pressure is greater. (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Euplagia
quadripunctaria

This is a day-flying moth, widely distributed in the Iberian Peninsula and common in the Mediterranean region, although in Portugal its distribution is clearly fragmented.

According to Maravalhas et al. (forthcoming), the species occurs in scattered, localized populations in the northern half of Portugal, where it inhabits mountainous areas. In the south it is confined to mountainous areas with preserved forests, such as the Arrábida, Monchique, and São Mamede ranges. It is present in the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation, probably occurring across much of the SAC in patches with suitable biotopes (ICNF, 2019). It uses a wide variety of habitats, preferably in areas associated with watercourses and tree and shrub vegetation. It is common in cool, shady, humid biotopes.
The caterpillar feeds on various herbaceous plants and woody shrubs and trees such as hazel (Corylus avellana), broom shrubs (Cytisus spp.), oaks (Quercus sp.), and honeysuckles (Lonicera sp.). Adults are flower-visiting.
The main threats are associated with the destruction of the preferred habitat, resulting from the removal of riparian vegetation and the consequent reduction of shelter, breeding, and feeding sites. If vegetation is cut during the larval development period, it can lead to local extinction. The introduction or spread of non-native plants promotes competition, reducing the availability of native host plants.
Pollution, resulting from the increased use of pesticides and fertilizers, reduces the species’ host plants and may also poison individuals, with greater impact on the larval stages. (ICNF, 2019). (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Oxygastra
Curtisii

A dragonfly with limited global distribution, it appears to be common, though locally distributed, in the Iberian Peninsula.

In Portugal it has mainly been recorded in the northern half of the country, where greater continuity between populations can also be seen, whereas in the south the species is restricted to small nuclei. (De Knijf & Demolder, 2010; De las Heras et al., 2017). It is present in the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation, probably occurring across much of the SAC in patches with suitable biotopes (ICNF, 2019). It occurs mainly in permanent lotic environments, in medium to large watercourses with muddy or sandy bottoms and abundant riparian vegetation, which provides shade and shelter in the edge zones (Torralba-Burrial et al., 2012b). It may also occur in small reservoirs, lakes, and slow-flowing channels, where it usually feeds. Females seek edge zones with roots and stones for egg-laying. Muddy bottoms in calm-water areas near the banks are important for larval development. (Dommanget, 1987; Pérez Gordillo, 2010b). The main threat to the species’ conservation is essentially linked to the destruction and fragmentation of its preferred habitat, resulting from human activities that alter the structure of the ecosystems in which it lives (such as work on watercourses and their banks, construction of dams, water abstraction) or that compromise water quality and/or the availability of trophic resources, such as water pollution. (Pérez Gordillo, 2010b; ICNF, 2019). Collection for private collections may be an additional threat (Pérez Gordillo, 2010b), although its importance in Portugal is unknown. (ICNF, 2019). (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Macromia
Splendens

Adults feed in clearings and forest roads, where they find various winged insects, while the larvae feed on aquatic macroinvertebrates and other insect larvae.

Macromia splendens is a dragonfly endemic to southern France and the northwest of the Iberian Peninsula. (Amorín et al., 2008c).
In Portugal it has mainly been recorded in the northern half of the country, where there is also greater continuity between populations, whereas in the south the species occurs in isolated clusters within limited areas. (Malkmus, 2002; De las Heras et al., 2017). It is present in the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation, probably occurring across much of the SAC in patches with suitable biotopes (ICNF, 2019).
It occurs mainly in permanent lotic environments, in stretches of wide, calm rivers of varying depth, with sunny areas and abundant vegetation along the banks. (Amorin et al., 2008c; Pérez Gordillo, 2010c; Leipelt & Suhling, 2005). Larval development occurs in muddy bottoms with decomposing plant material. (Pérez Gordillo, 2010c; Leipelt & Suhling, 2005). The species may occasionally colonize small weirs and dams. (Dommanget & Grand, 1996; Cordero Rivera, 2000). The main threat to the species’ conservation is essentially linked to the destruction and fragmentation of its preferred habitat, resulting from human activities that alter the structure of the ecosystems in which it lives, such as work on watercourses and their banks, construction of dams, water abstraction, or that compromise water quality and/or the availability of trophic resources, such as water pollution. (Pérez Gordillo, 2010c; ICNF, 2019). Changes to the structure of its habitat can lead to the local disappearance of the species. (ICNF, 2014a). (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Amphibians

There are 7 amphibian species associated with watercourses, with 3 Iberian endemics of particular note:

The Lusitanian salamander (Chioglossa lusitanica), classified as Vulnerable, Bosca’s newt (Lissotriton boscai), and the Iberian frog (Rana iberica).

The midwife toad (Alytes obstetricans) has also been recorded; it is included in Annex B-IV of the Habitats Directive and gets its name from the fact that the male carries the eggs on its back.

The Lusitanian salamander is a Vulnerable species found only in the Iberian Peninsula, where it is endemic. It is a slender amphibian with a small head, prominent eyes, and short, thin limbs. It is black on its dorsal side, with two copper-coloured longitudinal stripes that join on the long tail, while the belly is dark grey with white speckling. It can reach 15 cm in length. It is found in habitats with high relative humidity and mild temperatures, along mountain stream banks with clear, well-oxygenated water, riparian vegetation and near deciduous woodlands and wet meadows or other biotopes. As an adult it feeds on insects, spiders and small molluscs, and during the larval stage on small aquatic insects, molluscs and crustaceans.
Spring and autumn are the best times of year to observe it.

Mammals

The existence of plants with edible seeds and fruits provides food for 35 mammal species.

Among the many mammals are the European hedgehog (Erinaceus europaeus), the greater white-toothed shrew (Crocidura russula), the mole, the Iberian hare (Lepus granatensis), the European rabbit (Oryctolagus cuniculus), the red squirrel (Sciurus vulgaris), the weasel (Mustela nivalis), the European polecat (Mustela putorius), the stone marten (Martes foina), the European badger (Meles meles), the common genet (Genetta genetta), the Egyptian mongoose (Herpestes ichneumon), the red fox (Vulpes vulpes), the wild boar (Sus scrofa), 11 bat species, and a considerable number of small rodents.

Nine species are included in Annex III of the Bern Convention, the European hedgehog (Erinaceus europaeus), the greater white-toothed shrew (Crocidura russula), the Iberian hare (Lepus granatensis), the red squirrel (Sciurus vulgaris), the weasel (Mustela nivalis), the stone marten (Martes foina), the European badger (Meles meles), the common genet (Genetta genetta) and the Egyptian mongoose (Herpestes ichneumon). Bats are especially noteworthy, because almost all of them are covered by various protection statuses, making them particularly important from a conservation perspective. One of the most notable is Bechstein’s bat (Myotis bechsteinii), considered “Endangered” in the Red Book of the Vertebrates of Portugal and “Vulnerable” on the IUCN Red List.

Myotis bechsteinii is a species with a broad global distribution. In mainland Portugal there are few records of the species, and most of these are in the central area, with sporadic recent records in the north and south. Typically arboreal, it roosts mainly in tree cavities and is generally associated with mature, well-developed broadleaf forests. In northern and central Europe it hibernates in mines and caves and appears to breed almost exclusively in tree cavities. It feeds mainly in woods, preferably deciduous woodland with old trees, but may also forage in other forest types and even in agroforestry woodland systems. Isolated individuals may shelter in underground refuges, particularly during hibernation. Recent studies indicate that it uses underground refuges for mating. The most recent records in the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation refer to two captures in Mata da Margaraça in 2005 and 2006. As with the rest of the country, information on the species is scarce, and only limited distribution data is known. In Portugal it is classified as Endangered and considered very rare, with a population of fewer than 1000 individuals. The main threats include habitat degradation, due to the reduction and deterioration of mature native broadleaf woodland, and the reduction in the availability of prey, due to factors such as landscape change, changes in agricultural practices, pesticide use, and pollution of water bodies used as feeding areas. (taken from the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation Management Plan).

Birds

The diversity of biotopes enables the presence of a large number of birds.

The birds include a considerable number of interesting species. In Serra do Açor one can observe birds such as Montagu’s harrier (Circus pygargus), the European goshawk (Accipiter gentilis), the short-eared owl (Asio flammeus), the garden warbler (Sylvia borin), the Iberian chiffchaff (Phylloscopus ibericus) and the common kingfisher (Alcedo atthis). Particularly noteworthy from this group are Montagu’s harrier (Circus pygargus) and the short-eared owl (Asio flammeus), which appears in the Red Book of the Vertebrates of Portugal with the status of “Endangered”, and the European goshawk (Accipiter gentilis), together with the garden warbler (Sylvia borin), which are listed in the same document as “Vulnerable”. Also mentioned is an Iberian endemic, the Iberian chiffchaff (Phylloscopus ibericus). Other species living here include the tawny owl (Strix aluco), the black wheatear (Oenanthe leucura), the common buzzard (Buteo buteo), and the wood pigeon (Columba palumbus), some of which are also included in the Red Book of the Vertebrates of Portugal.

The European goshawk (Accipiter gentilis) is a bird of prey with a strong body, powerful wings, and a long tail. The male has bluish plumage, while the female is greyish on the dorsal side and whitish on the ventral side (with grey stripes) and a white stripe above the eyes. It can reach 64 cm in length and 1.2 m in wingspan, with females being larger. It inhabits forested areas in river valleys or flatter zones, near open land such as scattered scrubland, agricultural crops, and pastures. A carnivore, it feeds on birds, mammals, and reptiles.
It can be observed year-round. Larger than the hawk, it is also distinguished by its longer tail and projecting head. It is represented in the symbol of the Serra do Açor Protected Landscape.
