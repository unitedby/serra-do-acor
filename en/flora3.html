The Central Mountain Range marks the boundary between several biogeographical regions, where Mediterranean species coexist with more markedly Atlantic species. These influences can be seen, for example, in the difference between N-NW-facing slopes, where species with Atlantic and Eurosiberian distributions predominate (pedunculate oak, Pyrenean oak, sweet chestnut, broom, etc.), and S-SE-facing slopes, where Mediterranean plants predominate (strawberry tree, cork oak, holm oak, Cistus spp., etc.). Of the total recorded species, 30% are Mediterranean, 18% have Atlantic affinities, and 19% are endemic species (Iberian, Lusitanian, etc.).

History of the
Serra do Açor woodland

During the Tertiary period, subtropical vegetation predominated in our territory.

Between 5 and 3 million years ago, this vegetation, in the more inland mountain areas, was dominated by evergreen lauriform species (species with evergreen leaves resembling the bay laurel). Some of the dominant species belonged to genera such as Sequoia and Sweetgum, and species such as the common rhododendron (Rhododendron ponticum) and the Portugal laurel (Prunus lusitanica subsp. lusitanica) could be found.

About 3 million years ago, summer rainfall gradually decreased, leading to the establishment of a Mediterranean climate. Around this time, plants belonging to more Mediterranean genera began to dominate: Olea (the olive genus), Pistacia, Phillyrea (green olive tree), and Quercus (oaks)... 1.8 million years ago, the glaciations of the Quaternary period began. The cold, dry climate favoured vegetation similar to that found in the steppes, dominated by herbaceous species and some shrubs (such as heathers). However, in sheltered valleys of mountainous areas, some relics of the Tertiary forest survived. Portugal laurel is one of those relic species. Oaks are also thought to have taken refuge in these more sheltered valleys. Only about 10 000 years ago, with the end of the glaciations, did forest species begin to spread from these more sheltered areas. From then on, our territory became covered by a forest dominated by oaks.

However, this forest soon showed signs of the human activities that were beginning to develop. About 9000 years ago, with the Neolithic revolution, agriculture and pastoralism were “invented”.
From then on, over thousands of years, human beings took land from the primitive forest, usually by fire, to convert it into agricultural land or pasture for domestic animals. Closer to our time, between the 12th and 17th centuries, we practically destroyed all our native forest. Alongside pastoralism and agriculture came charcoal production and shipbuilding: building a single ship required the felling of thousands of oaks. As such, the forest that reached the 20th century was rather poor, and much of the country was covered by scrubland.
The large-scale plantations of maritime pine and eucalyptus that nowadays characterize our landscape, are the result of reforestation work carried out during the second half of the 20th century. However, these stands of trees have nothing to do with the forest that naturally fits our climatic conditions.

Threatened
species

Flora species belonging to 71 different families have been recorded in Protected Habitat PPSA 336.

Among the plant species present in this Protected Area are species with a threatened status according to the Red Book of the Vascular Flora of Mainland Portugal: Epipactis fageticola – Endangered; Neottia nidus-avis (L.) Rich. – Vulnerable; Lilium martagon – Vulnerable; Veronica micrantha – Near Threatened; Prunus lusitanica subsp. lusitanica – Near Threatened. The number of flora species included in the various annexes of the Habitats Directive indicates the importance of Serra do Açor flora for nature conservation at the international level. These include species such as Murbeckiella sousae Rothm. (Annex IV), Scrophularia grandiflora DC. (Annex V), Veronica micrantha Hoffmanns. & Link (Annex II), Festuca summilusitana Franco & Rocha Afonso (Annex II), Ruscus aculeatus L. (Annex V), Narcissus bulbocodium L. subsp. bulbocodium (Annex V), and Narcissus triandrus L. subsp. pallidulus (Graells) Rivas Goday (Annex IV).

Veronica
Micrantha

Veronica micrantha is a small herbaceous plant with tiny pink flowers, an endemic species of the northwestern Iberian quadrant.

In Portugal it is scattered across the Northern and Beiras regions, and is more abundant in mountainous areas. It is a characteristic species of moist woodland edges, especially in riparian areas and along the edges of wet meadows, and it also occurs, apparently spreading, along rural paths where water flows. The national trend of this species is uncertain, however, the SAC (Special Area of Conservation) is included in one of the territories where this species develops favourably, the Serra do Açor. (Carapeto et al., 2020). Within the SAC (Special Area of Conservation) it is only known to occur in the Serra do Açor Protected Landscape area. In 2010, 31 population clusters were recorded in Mata da Margaraça itself, totalling 282 specimens, and there was 1 cluster on the edge of former agricultural land near Pardieiros with 268 individual specimens, giving an estimated population of 550 individuals (taken from the Management Plan Report for the Complexo do Açor Special Area of Conservation).

Oak woodland —
Mata da Margaraça

In Serra do Açor, the most common oak species is the pedunculate oak, also known as the common oak, but you can also see others, such as the Pyrenean oak, the cork oak, and the holm oak.

The forest that would dominate the landscape after the end of the last glaciation (about 10 000 years ago) was dominated by oaks. Oak woods are forests which are very rich in species. Mata da Margaraça is a small part of that vast oak forest that once covered this mountain range. Small and rare relics of the primitive forest of this region survive in pockets of the mountain range: oak woodland, dominated by the pedunculate oak, also known as the common oak (Quercus robur) and accompanied by a large retinue of plant species (sweet chestnuts, elms, Portugal laurel, hollies, hazels, laurustinus, strawberry trees…).

Mata da Margaraça is the best-preserved example of this forest. The tree layer is dominated by pedunculate oak, also known as common oak (Quercus robur) and sweet chestnut (Castanea sativa), with some very old specimens of both species. Although less abundant, there are other deciduous species such as hazels, elms, cherry trees and walnut trees. This woodland is characterized by the presence of Mediterranean elements, such as the strawberry tree (Arbutus unedo), the laurustinus (Viburnum tinus) and the bay laurel (Laurus nobilis).
Its flora is very interesting, and Mata da Margaraça holds the largest known population of Portugal laurel (Prunus lusitanica subsp. lusitanica) across its entire distribution area. This species is a relic of the subtropical laurel forest of the Tertiary period. Also noteworthy is a small population of the rare Iberian endemic Veronica micrantha. Among the herbaceous species that overwinter as bulbs are interesting Portuguese flora species, such as the Martagon lily or Turk’s-cap lily (Lilium martagon), Solomon’s seal (Polygonatum odoratum), the daffodil Angel’s Tears (Narcissus triandrus), the sea holly species Durieu’s eryngo (Eryngium duriaei), and several species of orchids, such as the Narrow-leaved Helleborine (Cephalanthera longifolia) and the Early-purple Orchid (Orchis mascula).

A large number of species have their preferred habitat here: These include 249 vascular plant species, 98 moss species, 53 liverworts, 257 fungi species and, among the fauna, 279 species, of which 194 are butterflies, 5 amphibians, 8 reptiles, 39 birds and 32 mammals. In this type of forest, the tree and shrub layers are dense, creating a dark, humid environment with smaller temperature variations than outside the woodland. This is the main reason why the herb layer is poor in diversity and is made up mostly of species adapted to humid conditions and low light. This helps explain the presence of 17 different species of fern. Herbaceous species have also adapted in other ways to these conditions. For example, Primula acaulis is called “Primrose” because it is among the first to flower, at the end of winter, when the climate has become milder and plenty of light reaches the ground because the leaves of deciduous trees have not yet emerged. The dynamic balance of a deciduous forest is complex and includes countless interaction processes among all the living beings that are to be found there, whether between individual members of the same species or different species, and between these and the abiotic components of the environment (soil chemistry and physics, temperature, humidity, light, climate, etc.). From the importance of the organic matter present in dying organisms, to the main agents of decomposition (bacteria and fungi), passing through the entire food chain that begins with plants, which capture solar energy in the forest system through photosynthesis, and ends with predators such as the fox, the genet, and birds of prey, there are countless examples of these interactions. In the forest, each living being and each species has its indispensable place in the balance of the whole. Forests are also essential for soil formation and maintenance, climate regulation, regulation of the water cycle and nutrients. Without forests, the atmosphere would not be purified of carbon dioxide, and many species of living beings would lose the possibility of survival because they are adapted to this type of habitat.

Riparian
vegetation

These plant communities accompany much of the watercourses of Serra do Açor, forming riparian woodland along their banks.

They often occupy the bottoms of steep valleys, where the effects of forest fires have not been as severe.

The dominant species in the riparian woodland of Serra do Açor are willows (Salix atrocinerea and Salix salviifolia), the Portugal laurel (Prunus lusitanica subsp. lusitanica), and the bay laurel (Laurus nobilis). Other species include the elder (Sambucus nigra), the alder buckthorn (Frangula alnus), the alder (Alnus glutinosa), the laurustinus (Viburnum tinus), the strawberry tree (Arbutus unedo), the holly (Ilex aquifolium) and the hawthorn (Crataegus monogyna). In the shrub and herbaceous layers, various species appear. Along the watercourses, it is still possible to observe well-preserved corridors of native vegetation. The freshness of the water and the plants that grow here (species adapted to the proximity of running water) help these areas to be more protected from fire.

The shrub and herb layers contain species such as Oenanthe crocata, Saponaria officinalis, Erica arborea, Myosotis secunda, Erica lusitanica, Apium nodiflorum, Bryonia dioica, Hedera helix, Rubus sp., Tamus communis, Asplenium onopteris, Athyrium filix-femina, Blechnum spicant, Dryopteris affinis, Osmunda regalis, Polystichum setiferum, Luzula sylvatica subsp. henriquesii, Scrophularia scorodonia, Saxifraga spathularis and Euphorbia dulcis.

The presence of the alder (Alnus glutinosa) accompanied by species such as the holly (Ilex aquifolium), the bay laurel (Laurus nobilis), Luzula sylvatica subsp. henriquesii, Scrophularia scorodonia, Saxifraga spathularis, and Euphorbia dulcis indicates this is a *91EO habitat of the Habitats Directive (a priority habitat for conservation).

The sometimes abundant presence of Portugal laurels, a species of great scientific and ornamental interest with a restricted distribution area made up of small relict populations, contributes to the high conservation value of these communities. Portugal laurel is also a good indicator of relict ecosystems, preferred habitats for many species, especially bryophytes. Riparian corridors also function as ecological corridors.

As mentioned, these communities follow watercourses, often at the bottom of steep valleys where the effects of forest fires have not been as intense as on adjacent slopes. They therefore function as refuges for fauna, facilitating movement and shelter for numerous species, and also providing shady, humid microhabitats necessary for the development of many plant species. This community also plays an important role in preventing erosion, thereby contributing to soil formation and retention. The role of riparian corridors as barriers that hinder the spread of fires is also noteworthy.

Portugal laurel Prunus lusitanica
subsp. lusitanica

The Portugal laurel (Prunus lusitanica subsp. lusitanica) holds a prominent place among the species in the Mata da Margaraça.

It is a species of considerable scientific and ornamental interest, with a restricted distribution area and it is an indicator of relict communities and ecosystems important for biodiversity conservation. According to some authors, Serra do Açor holds the largest populations of Portugal laurel across its entire distribution range, with Mata da Margaraça possessing the largest existing population.

This species was part of the subtropical forest that covered this territory for many millions of years during the Tertiary period, and later survived the Quaternary glaciations in more sheltered valleys. Sixty-five million years ago, when the climate was warmer and wetter and long before the glaciers covered much of what is now mainland Portugal, there was a forest here that can be called the Laurissilva, since it was dominated by laurel-like species, that is, species with the broad and glossy leaves similar to those of the bay laurel.

Its current natural range extends from the western Pyrenees to the mountains of Morocco, with its optimal distribution occurring in the Iberian Peninsula. In Portugal it occurs in Gerês and in the mountainous zones of the central mountain range, including Estrela, Açor, and Lousã, extending south to the municipality of Mação. Its attractive evergreen foliage gives the species strong ornamental potential, and it has been used in emblematic gardens such as those of Queluz National Palace and that at Parque das Nações.
This potential is confirmed at flowering time by the exuberance of countless magnificent and fragrant clusters of white flowers that delight bees and many other pollinating insects. According to the Red List of the Vascular Flora of Mainland Portugal, the Portugal laurel is classified as Near Threatened.
Portugal laurel stands constitute a Priority Habitat for Nature Conservation at the European level (Habitat code 5230 under the Habitats Directive, transposed into Portuguese legislation through Decree-Law No. 140/99, of 24 April).
