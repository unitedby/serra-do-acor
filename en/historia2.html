The unique character imposed by the mountain’s physical shape determined certain aspects of local life, as people used its resources across its different slopes, making full use of the forest’s multiple functions.

Scattered villages mean that the predominantly schist-built houses in some places cluster together, lining up along watercourses, while in others they hide away tucked into the mountain’s folds. The distinctive crafts make use of local resources and add value to existing heritage, through wooden spoons, miniature schist houses, and baskets. There is also limited production of wooden troughs and clogs. Agriculture and pastoralism now play a minor role in the mountain range. Honey remains a recognized product, valued for its quality and as a source of income. In the valleys, some family farms survive in small areas near settlements and along watercourses. In the mountain villages, where the slopes are very steep, agricultural land appears as terraced plots built with schist walls to retain the soil, creating a singular and striking landscape.

At the bottom of the valleys there was the patchwork agricultural space, sometimes green, sometimes brown, reflecting the rhythm of crops throughout the year. Moving up the slopes were the brushwood and scrublands that provided firewood, grazing, and bedding material for livestock; in the higher areas, large tracts were used for extensive grazing. Settlements were located in the flattest areas, which had good sun exposure and which were close to watercourses. In addition, buildings were erected on rocky outcrops, freeing the more fertile soils for agricultural use. The enrichment of the landscape with built forms extended through walls, irrigation channels, cobbled paths, small bridges, water mills, shepherd shelters and roadside shrines...
