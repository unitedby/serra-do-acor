Serra do Açor

Forest fires

PT

·

EN

The burning of forest to clear land for agriculture and pastoralism goes back to the first moments in human history with an impact on the destruction of the native forest. Evidence of grazing and cereal cultivation began to appear in the region around 7600 years ago. Around 2400 years ago, the landscape was already only semi-wooded.

The lush native oak woodland was gradually replaced by low scrubland dominated by heather. Maritime pine plantations then appeared. Some 19th-century botanists already mention the presence of this species in Serra do Açor. However, large-scale pine plantations only took shape in the 1940s. The rural exodus of the 1960s and 1970s removed much of the population that had assured a certain management of these forest stands. The accumulation of combustible material, the large areas of pine monoculture, and the fact that pine is a resinous species, together with factors such as the climate and others, have led to fires that are too frequent and too intense, and which have been responsible for the destruction of vast areas.

This destruction of the vegetation cover by fire exposed the soil to the action of erosive agents. The steep slopes and numerous watercourses facilitated soil removal. Skeletal soils, or even the absence of soil, limits the ability of vegetation to develop on many slopes of this mountain range. Some invasive plant species, such as acacias, have expanded their territory thanks to such incidences of fire. The disappearance of native vegetation opens the way for the invasion of these fast-growing species, which in a short period of time can establish impenetrable stands hindering the natural regeneration of native communities.
